## Post Content

Some hotels are not sold out.

They just look sold out to the PMS.

This happens more often than people think.

A guest asks for three nights. The room type has empty nights across the calendar. But those nights are scattered across different physical rooms, so the PMS cannot see one clean stay to sell.

The hotel says no.

Not because demand is missing.

Not because price is wrong.

Because the calendar geometry is wrong.

That is the idea behind Smart Allocator: treat room assignment like Tetris.

Same rooms. Same confirmed bookings. Same prices.

But if you reshuffle bookings inside each room type, you can close single-night gaps and open more bookable 3-night windows.

That revenue is not invented.

It is recovered from demand the hotel was already capable of accepting, but the allocation pattern made invisible.

There is a second layer too: upgrades.

Moving one booking up can free cheaper-room availability, which helps in soft demand. But it can also consume premium inventory that may have sold later. So upgrades should be treated as expected-value decisions, not automatic generosity.

I built an interactive notebook to make both ideas visible:

https://blog.revenuetales.com/smart-allocator/

It lets you see how a simple reshuffle changes the number of sellable windows, and when an upgrade helps or hurts.

How often do you review allocation as a revenue lever, not just an operations task?
